# Barefoot Digital — Voice & Messaging Guide

*The verbal half of the brand system: how Barefoot Digital sounds in writing, across every context. Visual and UI direction are maintained separately. This is the layer that keeps copy consistent whether it's written by Henry, a contractor, or an AI assistant.*

## Who's speaking

Barefoot Digital is a solo operation — founded and run by Henry Barefoot, a senior full-stack engineer based in South Florida — but it writes at two deliberate registers, and picking the right one is the first decision in any piece of copy.

**Company voice ("we")** is the default for anything institutional: the website, proposals, SOWs, legal documents, marketing, the partner application. Plural but never corporate — confident, credible, plain. "We help teams put AI to work," not "Barefoot Digital is pleased to offer synergistic solutions."

**Founder voice ("I" / "— Henry")** is for direct human contact: the assessment delivery, follow-up emails, personal notes, LinkedIn posts. Warm, peer-to-peer, signs off as Henry. It's what makes a one-person company feel like a person rather than a brand.

The rule: if a *system* is talking — a contract, a landing page, a policy — use "we." If *Henry* is talking to someone — an email, a post, a reply — use "I." The more personal the moment, the more it leans founder voice.

## The voice in five traits

**1. Honest to a fault.** The load-bearing trait — everything else sits on it. Claims are earned, not asserted. No trust badges, no borrowed credibility, no number we can't source. We state limitations openly, because in a category full of overselling, candor is the differentiator. When the assessment said "where AI can help your team — and where it can't yet," the "can't yet" was the point.

**2. Operator to operator.** The reader is a smart professional — an HR, L&D, IT, or operations leader — and we write to them as a peer, not a prospect. Respect their intelligence, skip the 101, explain the *why* behind a recommendation. The founder is an engineer, so the copy sounds like someone who's actually built the thing — because he has.

**3. Concrete over claimed.** Show the shipped thing. Evidence beats adjectives every time. "In production for Allied Yacht Transport" does more than "industry-leading results." "A memory layer for AI agents, shipped on npm" does more than "revolutionary AI technology." If a sentence leans on a superlative, swap the superlative for the proof.

**4. Plain and direct.** Short sentences. Active voice. Lead with the point. Cut the filler. Clarity *is* the brand — the same discipline that makes good software makes good copy. If a reader has to re-read a sentence, rewrite it.

**5. Calibrated, not breathless.** Specifically about AI: sober optimism. AI is a tool with a fit and real limits — not magic, not a threat. We sell enablement *and* say where AI falls short, and that honesty is exactly what earns trust from buyers who've been pitched hype by everyone else.

*Underneath all five runs a quiet confidence with a light, dry wit — "shelfware," "shadow AI," "that's the point." Earned, never quirky for its own sake. Personality, not performance.*

## Claims & substantiation — the honesty rules, made operational

The first trait is important enough to be an editorial policy, not just a vibe. These are hard rules:

- **Every metric is sourced, or it's cut.** No unattributed percentages, no "up to X%" with nothing behind it. If you cite it, you can show where it came from.
- **No badges, seals, or "X-compliant" graphics.** No certification makes a footer badge true, and self-asserted seals read as amateur to a B2B buyer. State the practice in plain words instead.
- **Never borrow credibility you haven't earned.** Don't imply certifications, partnerships, or attestations the company doesn't hold. If a prospect's security team needs proof of an underlying provider's control, point them to that provider's actual attestation — don't reskin it as ours.
- **Attributions stay accurate.** If a project used OpenAI, it used OpenAI — we don't relabel it to fit whatever the current stack story is. Accuracy outranks tidiness.
- **State limitations and tradeoffs.** Saying what something *doesn't* do, or where it doesn't fit, builds more trust than hiding it — and it's on-brand.
- **Data and privacy claims are stated as practice, plainly, and only where true.** "We don't train AI models on client data." "Client data stays in your own infrastructure." These describe paid engagements — never imply the website itself collects nothing.

## Lexicon

**Reach for:** ship, build, enablement, readiness, adoption, use case, roadmap, practical, concrete, in production, operator, hands-on. Calibrated AI language — "where it fits," "what it can't do yet."

**Avoid:** revolutionary, cutting-edge, game-changing, transformative (as filler), synergy, seamless, robust, leverage (as a verb), unlock (overused), "AI-powered everything," empty superlatives, and fear-based framing ("don't get left behind," "adapt or die"). If a word would appear in every competitor's deck, cut it.

**How we talk about AI:** a tool with a fit and limits — not magic, not a savior, not a threat. The honesty about limits is a feature of the voice, not a hedge.

**Names & entity:** Products are capitalized as proper nouns — Engram, Paw, Frutero. Use **Barefoot Digital** everywhere public. Use the full legal name — **Astral Events Miami LLC d/b/a Barefoot Digital** — only in legal and financial documents, verbatim. Don't surface the legal entity in marketing copy.

## House style

- **Headings:** sentence case ("What your score means," not "What Your Score Means").
- **Em dashes** are welcome — they carry asides and set rhythm. Used deliberately, they're part of the voice.
- **Oxford comma:** yes.
- **Numbers:** be specific, and if you cite one, you can source it.
- **CTAs:** verb-first and low-pressure — "Book a call," "Get the assessment," "Download the assessment." No exclamation-point urgency, no manufactured scarcity.
- **Sign-off:** "— Henry" in founder voice.
- **Emoji:** none in marketing, legal, or proposal copy; fine sparingly in casual founder channels (a LinkedIn post, a reply) when it's natural, not decorative.
- **Identity block:** legal and formal footers use "Astral Events Miami LLC d/b/a Barefoot Digital · St. Petersburg, FL · barefootdigital.dev"; marketing footers use "Barefoot Digital."

## Register by context

| Context | How it sounds |
|---|---|
| Website | Confident, plain, capability-led. Leads with what we do for the reader; proof, not adjectives. |
| Proposals / SOWs | Precise and professional, still human. Clear scope, no legalese theater. |
| Legal documents | Formal, exact, no flourish. Plain enough to actually read. |
| Transactional email | Brief and warm, founder voice. Deliver the thing, one soft next step. |
| Sales / follow-up email | Personal, peer-to-peer, zero pressure. Henry talking, not a sequence. |
| LinkedIn / social | Founder voice, opinionated, teaching. A point of view, not announcements. |
| Course / educational | Instructive and patient. Makes complex things legible — the teaching instinct that runs through the whole brand. |
| Partner application | Credible and evidence-led, plural "we." Show the shipped stack and the real work. |

## Boilerplate

Canonical descriptions to reuse verbatim, so the company describes itself the same way everywhere. *(Pick the category noun you prefer — "studio," "practice," or "company" — and keep it consistent.)*

**One-liner:** Barefoot Digital helps organizations put AI to work — practical enablement, training, and tooling for teams adopting AI.

**Short (bio / footer / partner app):** Barefoot Digital is an AI enablement [company] for B2B teams. We help HR, L&D, IT, and operations leaders adopt AI through hands-on consulting, training, and tools we build ourselves — including Engram, a memory layer for AI agents, and Paw, an agent framework.

**Standard paragraph (about / proposals):** Barefoot Digital helps organizations move from talking about AI to using it. We work with HR, L&D, IT, and operations teams on practical adoption — finding the high-value use cases, building the skills, and putting the right tooling in place. Alongside client work we build our own products, including Engram (persistent memory for AI agents) and Paw (an agent framework), and we run our own self-hosted stack — so the guidance we give comes from systems we actually operate.

**Founder line:** Founded and run by Henry Barefoot, a senior full-stack engineer based in South Florida.

*All boilerplate is held to the honesty rules: no client counts, results, or claims that aren't true and current.*

## Do / don't

**Trust & compliance**
- Don't: a "GDPR · CCPA Compliant" shield badge in the footer.
- Do: "GDPR & CCPA rights respected · We don't train AI models on client data · Client data stays in your own infrastructure" — plain text, no seal.
- *Why:* no certification makes a badge true; the plain statement is both honest and more credible.

**Capability**
- Don't: "Industry-leading AI solutions delivering transformative results."
- Do: "In production for Allied Yacht Transport." · "Engram — a memory layer for AI agents, shipped on npm."
- *Why:* name the proof; adjectives are free, shipped work isn't.

**Metrics**
- Don't: "95% efficiency improvement." (no source)
- Do: attribute it — what was measured, across what — or cut it.

**AI framing**
- Don't: "Don't get left behind — AI is transforming everything."
- Do: "Find where AI can help your team — and where it can't yet."
- *Why:* calibrated beats fear; candor about limits is the differentiator.

**Borrowed credibility**
- Don't: "Built on SOC 2-certified infrastructure." (when we can't verify or don't hold it)
- Do: state only what's true and verifiable; route security questions to the provider's real attestation.
